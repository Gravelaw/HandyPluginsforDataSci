## Executive Deck Framework

- Selected frameworks: Pyramid Principle for answer-first logic, MECE grouping for executive structure, Gestalt/visual hierarchy for fast scanning, and WCAG accessibility for credible boardroom readability.
- Use answer-first logic: conclusion, why it matters, proof, decision.
- Apply Pyramid Principle style grouping: one governing thought, 3-5 supporting reasons, appendix for detail.
- Build for scanning: 5-12 slides for a short executive deck unless the user requests otherwise.
- Every slide headline should be a decision-grade sentence, not a topic label.
- Keep details in speaker notes or appendix when they slow decision-making.
- Apply Gestalt proximity, similarity, contrast, and alignment so executives can see groupings, priority, and the decision path without reading every word.

## Design System

- Theme: polished, quiet, confident; no decorative clutter.
- Colors: one primary brand/anchor color, neutral canvas, one alert/accent color, accessible contrast.
- Fonts: large, readable, restrained; avoid mixing many families.
- Layout: fewer objects, stronger hierarchy, clear whitespace, direct chart labels.
- Charts: show only the comparison or trend needed for the decision.
- Icons: use sparingly for navigation, capabilities, or category markers.
- Smart graphics: use for strategy maps, maturity curves, decision trees, operating models, and roadmaps.
- Video: use rarely; if included, it must demonstrate product/customer evidence and have a fallback still.

## Executive QA Gates

- Can the main message be understood in 30 seconds?
- Is the ask or decision explicit?
- Are the key numbers exact, sourced, and unit-consistent?
- Does every chart have a takeaway in the title?
- Is the appendix separated from the core narrative?
- Does the deck look credible at boardroom scale?
